⭕ Circles

Master circles step by step — from basic parts all the way to sectors, arcs, and composite shapes.

🟢

Easy Concepts

Level 1
1Parts of a Circle
  • Center (•)The middle point of the circle — every point on the circle is the same distance from it.
  • Radius (r)Distance from the center to any point on the circle's edge.
  • Diameter (d)Distance all the way across the circle through the center. d = 2r
  • Circumference (C)The total distance all the way around the circle (its perimeter).
2Circumference Formula
C = π × d  =  2 × π × r
π ≈ 3.14159  |  use π ≈ 3.14 for most problems
The circumference is like "unrolling" the circle into a straight line. That line's length equals π times the diameter. Since d = 2r, both forms mean the same thing.
Worked Example
d = 10 cm
C = π × d
C = 3.14 × 10
C = 31.4 cm
3Area of a Circle
Area = π × r²
r² means r × r  |  always use the radius, not diameter
The area tells us how much flat space is inside the circle. We square the radius (multiply it by itself), then multiply by π.
Worked Example
r = 5 cm
Area = π × r²
Area = 3.14 × 5²
Area = 3.14 × 25
Area = 78.5 cm²
4Diameter vs Radius
d = 2r     r = d ÷ 2
⚠ Common mistake: When a problem gives you the diameter, you must divide by 2 to get the radius before using the area formula. The area formula needs r, not d!
Worked Example — given diameter
d = 12 cm → r = 12 ÷ 2 = 6 cm
Area = π × r²
Area = 3.14 × 6²
Area = 3.14 × 36
Area = 113.04 cm²
5Estimating with π
π (pi) is a special number that goes on forever without repeating. For most 5th-grade problems:

• Use π ≈ 3.14 for standard calculations
• Use π ≈ 3.14159 when you need more precision
• Write the symbol π to give an exact answer (e.g., "50π cm²")
• Remember: π is slightly more than 3 and slightly less than 3.2
Quick Check
If a circle has r = 10, its area ≈ 3.14 × 100 = 314 cm². The answer should always be in the hundreds range for r = 10. Use this to sanity-check your work!

Medium Concepts

Level 2
1Finding Radius from Area
r = √(A ÷ π)
Rearranged from A = πr² → r² = A ÷ π → r = √(A ÷ π)
  • 1Divide area by π (÷ 3.14)
  • 2Take the square root of the result
  • 3That is your radius!
Worked Example
A = 78.5 cm²
r² = 78.5 ÷ 3.14 = 25
r = √25
r = 5 cm
2Finding Diameter from Circumference
d = C ÷ π     r = d ÷ 2
Both come from rearranging C = πd
  • 1Divide circumference by π (÷ 3.14)
  • 2Result is the diameter
  • 3Halve it if you need the radius
Worked Example
C = 62.8 m
d = 62.8 ÷ 3.14 = 20 m
r = 20 ÷ 2 = 10 m
3Semicircle — Area & Perimeter
Area = πr² ÷ 2
Perimeter = πr + 2r  (curved arc + straight diameter)
A semicircle is exactly half a circle. Its area is half the full circle area. Its perimeter has two parts: the curved arc (half the circumference = πr) plus the flat diameter (2r).
Worked Example — r = 7 cm
Area = 3.14 × 7² ÷ 2
= 3.14 × 49 ÷ 2
= 153.86 ÷ 2
= 76.93 cm²

Perimeter = πr + 2r
= 3.14 × 7 + 14
= 21.98 + 14
= 35.98 cm
4Quarter Circle
Area = πr² ÷ 4
Arc length = πr ÷ 2  |  Perimeter = πr/2 + 2r
A quarter circle is one-fourth of a full circle. Divide full-circle area and circumference by 4 to get the quarter-circle equivalents. The perimeter includes the arc plus the two straight radii.
Worked Example — r = 8 cm
Area = 3.14 × 8² ÷ 4
= 3.14 × 64 ÷ 4
= 200.96 ÷ 4
= 50.24 cm²

Arc = 3.14 × 8 ÷ 2 = 12.56 cm
Perim = 12.56 + 8 + 8 = 28.56 cm
5Annulus (Ring Shape)
Area = π(R² − r²)
R = outer radius  |  r = inner radius
An annulus is a ring — a big circle with a hole cut from the center. Its area is the outer circle's area minus the inner circle's area.
Worked Example — R = 10, r = 6
Area = π(R² − r²)
= 3.14 × (100 − 36)
= 3.14 × 64
= 200.96 cm²
6Real-World Applications
Circles appear everywhere in real life. The key is to identify what you're given (r or d?) and what the question asks for (area or circumference?).
Three Quick Examples
🍕 Pizza (r=15 cm):
  Area = 3.14 × 225 = 706.5 cm²

🛞 Wheel (d=70 cm) — distance per rotation:
  C = 3.14 × 70 = 219.8 cm per rotation

🏊 Pool (r=8 m) — tiling the floor:
  Area = 3.14 × 64 = 200.96 m²

Hard Concepts

Level 3
1Sector Area (Pie Slice)
Area = (θ ÷ 360) × π × r²
θ = angle of the sector in degrees
A sector is a "pie slice" of a circle. The fraction θ/360 tells you what fraction of the whole circle you have. Multiply that fraction by the full circle's area.
Worked Example — θ = 90°, r = 6 cm
Fraction = 90 ÷ 360 = ¼
Full area = 3.14 × 6² = 3.14 × 36 = 113.04
Sector area = ¼ × 113.04
= 28.26 cm²
2Arc Length
Arc length = (θ ÷ 360) × 2πr
Same fraction idea — but applied to the full circumference
The arc length is the distance along the curved edge of a sector. It is the same fraction (θ/360) of the full circumference (2πr).
Worked Example — θ = 120°, r = 9 cm
Fraction = 120 ÷ 360 = ⅓
Full circumference = 2 × 3.14 × 9 = 56.52 cm
Arc length = ⅓ × 56.52
= 18.84 cm
3Perimeter of a Sector
Perimeter = arc length + 2r
Arc length = (θ/360) × 2πr  — add the two straight radii
The boundary of a sector has three parts: the curved arc and the two straight radii. Add all three to get the perimeter.
Worked Example — θ = 90°, r = 6 cm
Arc = (90/360) × 2 × 3.14 × 6
= ¼ × 37.68
= 9.42 cm

Perimeter = arc + 2r
= 9.42 + 12
= 21.42 cm
4Composite Shapes with Circles
Total Area = rectangle + semicircle
= (l × w) + (πr² ÷ 2)
For "stadium" shapes (rectangle + semicircle on top), split into familiar parts. Calculate each part's area separately then add them together.

For the perimeter: two lengths + two semicircles = two lengths + one full circle's circumference.
Worked Example — 10×6 rect + semicircle r = 5
Rectangle area = 10 × 6 = 60 cm²
Semicircle area = 3.14 × 5² ÷ 2
= 3.14 × 25 ÷ 2
= 39.25 cm²
Total area = 60 + 39.25 = 99.25 cm²

Perimeter: 2 sides (6+6) + base (10) + arc (πr=15.7)
= 12 + 10 + 15.7 = 37.7 cm
5Area Between Shapes
Shaded area = Aouter − Ainner
Subtract the inner shape from the outer shape
When a circle sits inside another shape (or vice versa), the shaded region is always outer − inner. Use this strategy for any "hole" or "gap" problem.
Worked Example — 10×10 square, inscribed circle r = 5
Square area = 10 × 10 = 100 cm²
Circle area = 3.14 × 5² = 3.14 × 25 = 78.5 cm²
Shaded area = 100 − 78.5
= 21.5 cm²
6Challenging Multi-Step Problems
Problem: A circular track has an inner radius of 50 m and an outer radius of 56 m.
(a) Find the area of the track.
(b) Find the distance walked in 3 laps on the inner edge.
Full Step-by-Step Solution
(a) Track area = π(R² − r²)
= 3.14 × (56² − 50²)
= 3.14 × (3136 − 2500)
= 3.14 × 636
= 1997.04 m²

(b) Inner circumference (r=50):
C = 2 × 3.14 × 50
C = 314 m per lap
3 laps = 3 × 314
= 942 m
Strategy for Multi-Step Problems
1. Read carefully — identify all given values.
2. Label R (outer) vs r (inner) if there's a ring.
3. Solve each part separately — don't rush.
4. Check units: areas → m², distances → m.
