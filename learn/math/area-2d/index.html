Area of 2D Shapes

Area tells us how much surface a flat shape covers. It's measured in square units like cm², m², or km². Master every formula from easy rectangles all the way to tricky sectors and reverse problems!

🟢 Easy Concepts

Build your foundation — these are the shapes you'll use most often

1. Rectangle

Area = length × width

Multiply the two different side lengths together. The result is in square units — because you're covering a flat surface.

Worked Example A classroom is 8 m long and 5 m wide. Area = 8 × 5 = 40 m²

2. Square

Area = s × s = s²

A square has all sides equal, so multiply the side length by itself. The little ² means "squared" — multiplied by itself.

Worked Example A square floor tile has side length 4 cm. Area = 4 × 4 = 4² = 16 cm²

3. Triangle

Area = ½ × base × height

The height must be perpendicular (at 90°) to the base — shown by the dashed line. Think of it as half a parallelogram!

Tip: ½ × base × height is the same as (base × height) ÷ 2

Worked Example Base = 6 cm, height = 4 cm Area = ½ × 6 × 4 = ½ × 24 = 12 cm²

4. Parallelogram

Area = base × height

The height is the perpendicular distance between the two parallel sides — it is NOT the slanted side length!

Remember: always use the vertical height, not the slant!

Worked Example Base = 8 m, height = 3 m Area = 8 × 3 = 24 m²

5. Counting Squares

Count whole + ½ squares

Place the shape on squared paper:

  • Count every whole square inside the shape — each = 1 unit²
  • For half squares (cut diagonally), count as ½ unit²
  • Add them all up!
Worked Example Shape above: 9 whole squares + 1 half square Area = 9 + ½ = 9.5 cm²

6. Square Units & Conversions

1 m² = 10,000 cm²

Area is always measured in square units. When you convert lengths, areas change by the square of the scale factor.

  • 1 m = 100 cm, so 1 m² = 100 × 100 = 10,000 cm²
  • 1 km = 1,000 m, so 1 km² = 1,000 × 1,000 = 1,000,000 m²

Always write the unit in your final answer!

🟡 Medium Concepts

More complex shapes — take it one step at a time

7. Trapezoid

Area = ½ × (a + b) × h

A trapezoid has two parallel sides (called a and b). The formula averages the two parallel sides and multiplies by the height.

Tip: (a + b) ÷ 2 is the average width of the shape

Worked Example a = 5 cm, b = 9 cm, h = 4 cm Area = ½ × (5 + 9) × 4 = ½ × 14 × 4 = ½ × 56 = 28 cm²

8. Circle Area

Area = π × r² (π ≈ 3.14)

r is the radius — the distance from the centre to the edge. Square the radius first, then multiply by π.

Remember: diameter = 2r, so if given diameter, halve it first!

Worked Example Radius = 7 cm Area = π × 7² = 3.14 × 49 ≈ 153.86 cm²

9. Composite Shapes

Total Area = A + B + …

Split the shape into simple rectangles/triangles, calculate each area separately, then add them together.

  • Look for an L-shape, T-shape, or any irregular polygon
  • Draw lines to divide it into shapes you know
  • Find the missing dimensions from the overall size
  • Add all the areas
Worked Example Rectangle A = 100 × 70 = 7,000; Rectangle B = 215 × 60 = 12,900 Total = 19,900 units²

10. Shaded Region

Shaded = Big Area − Small Area

When a shape is cut out from another, find the big shape area first, then subtract the cut-out area.

  • Find the area of the outer (big) shape
  • Find the area of the inner (removed) shape
  • Shaded area = Big − Small
Worked Example Rectangle 10 × 8 = 80 cm². Circle r = 3: π×9 ≈ 28.26 cm² Shaded = 80 − 28.26 ≈ 51.74 cm²

11. Rhombus

Area = (d₁ × d₂) ÷ 2

A rhombus has four equal sides. Use its two diagonals (the lines connecting opposite corners). They always cross at right angles.

Diagonals of a rhombus always meet at 90°

Worked Example d₁ = 10 cm, d₂ = 6 cm Area = (10 × 6) ÷ 2 = 60 ÷ 2 = 30 cm²

12. Scale Drawings

Real Area = Drawing Area × scale²

In scale drawings, lengths are multiplied by the scale factor, but areas are multiplied by the scale factor squared.

  • Find the area in the drawing using normal formulae
  • Multiply by scale² (not just scale)
Worked Example Scale 1 : 100. Drawing shows 3 cm × 2 cm = 6 cm². Real area = 6 × 100² = 6 × 10,000 = 60,000 cm² = 6 m²

🔴 Hard Concepts

Challenge yourself — these need careful multi-step thinking

13. Path / Border Problems

Path Area = Outer Area − Inner Area

A border (path) runs around the inside of a shape. To find its area:

  • Find the outer rectangle area
  • Find the inner rectangle dimensions: subtract 2 × path width from each outer dimension
  • Inner area = inner length × inner width
  • Path area = Outer − Inner
Worked Example Outer = 12 × 8 = 96 m². Path width = 2 m. Inner = (12−4) × (8−4) = 8 × 4 = 32 m² Path = 96 − 32 = 64 m²

14. Irregular Polygons on a Coordinate Grid

Shoelace: A = ½|Σ(xᵢyᵢ₊₁ − xᵢ₊₁yᵢ)|

Or: decompose into triangles and rectangles

For polygons with vertices listed as coordinates, the Shoelace Formula gives the exact area.

  • List all vertices in order: (x₁,y₁), (x₂,y₂), …
  • Multiply diagonally: each x by the next y
  • Subtract: each y by the next x
  • Take half the absolute value of the total

Alternative: enclose in a rectangle and subtract the surrounding triangles/rectangles.

Simpler Method — Enclosing Rectangle Draw a bounding rectangle. Find its area. Subtract each triangle in the corners. Area of polygon = Rectangle − corner triangles

15. Sector of a Circle

Area = (θ ÷ 360) × π × r²

A sector is a "pizza slice" cut from a circle. θ (theta) is the angle at the centre in degrees. The fraction θ/360 tells you what fraction of the full circle you have.

  • Find the full circle area: π × r²
  • Multiply by the fraction of the circle: θ ÷ 360
Worked Example θ = 90°, r = 6 cm Area = (90 ÷ 360) × π × 36 = ¼ × 3.14 × 36 = ¼ × 113.04 ≈ 28.26 cm²

16. Mixed Unit Problems

Convert → then Calculate

When measurements are in different units, you must convert them all to the same unit before using any area formula.

  • 1 m = 100 cm, so 1 m² = 10,000 cm²
  • 1 km = 1,000 m, so 1 km² = 1,000,000 m²
  • Choose the most convenient unit (usually the one already given for most measurements)
  • Convert, calculate, then state the unit clearly
Worked Example Rectangle: length = 2 m, width = 50 cm Convert: 2 m = 200 cm Area = 200 × 50 = 10,000 cm² = 10,000 cm² = 1 m²

17. Perimeter vs Area

Same P ≠ Same Area

Perimeter is the total length around the outside of a shape. Area is the surface covered inside. These are independent — same perimeter can give very different areas.

Key insight: for a fixed perimeter, a square always gives the maximum possible area!

Key Fact Both shapes have perimeter = 20 units. Square 5×5: Area = 25 units² Rectangle 9×1: Area = 9 units² Square wins every time for max area!

18. Reverse Problems — Find a Missing Dimension

Missing side = Area ÷ known side

Sometimes you're given the area and one dimension, and asked to find the other. Rearrange the formula algebraically.

  • Write out the area formula
  • Substitute in what you know
  • Solve for the unknown using division (or square root for a square)
Worked Example 1 — Rectangle Area = 48 cm², length = 8 cm. Find width. 48 = 8 × width → width = 48 ÷ 8 width = 6 cm
Worked Example 2 — Square Area = 81 cm². Find the side. s² = 81 → s = √81 s = 9 cm
