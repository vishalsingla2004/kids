🟢 Easy
1. Active Listening (SL.5.1)
Active Listening Rules
Eyes on speaker · No interrupting · Ask questions

Active listening means giving your full attention. Look at the speaker, stay quiet while they talk, and think about what they are saying.

Example
During a class discussion, Maria puts her pencil down, looks at her classmate, and waits until they finish before raising her hand. That is active listening.
2. Main Idea of a Talk (SL.5.2)
Finding Main Idea
What is the speaker mostly talking about?

The main idea is the most important point of a speech or audio clip. Listen for repeated words or ideas — those clues point to the main idea.

Example
A speaker mentions recycling, reducing waste, and reusing containers three times each. Main idea: ways to protect the environment.
3. Key Details from Audio (SL.5.2)
Key Details Strategy
Who · What · When · Where · Why · How

Key details support the main idea. Use the 5W+H questions as a mental checklist while listening to capture the most important facts.

Example
From a podcast: "The blue whale (who) can travel (what) 1,000 miles (how much) in search of food (why)." Each part is a key detail.
4. Following Directions (SL.5.1)
Strategy
Listen → Restate → Execute in order

Multi-step directions require careful listening. Restate each step in your own words to check understanding before acting. Note sequence words: first, next, then, finally.

Example
"First fold the paper in half, then cut along the dotted line, finally unfold." — Restate: "Fold, cut dotted, unfold." Then do it in that order.
5. Speaking Clearly (SL.5.4)
Clear Speaking Checklist
Volume · Pace · Eye contact · Pronunciation

Effective speakers use appropriate volume (loud enough for everyone), moderate pace (not too fast), make eye contact, and pronounce words clearly.

Example
Instead of rushing through a book report, Jake spoke at a steady pace, looked up from his notes every few sentences, and said each word clearly. Result: everyone understood him.
6. Discussion Rules (SL.5.1)
Discussion Norms
Take turns · Build on ideas · Stay on topic

Good discussions follow rules: one person speaks at a time, everyone builds on what others say, and the group stays on the assigned topic.

Example
"I agree with Mia's point that wolves help the ecosystem. I want to add that they control deer populations too." — Building on an idea.
🟡 Medium
7. Summarize a Presentation (SL.5.2)
Summary Formula
Main idea + 2–3 key details (your own words)

A summary retells the most important information in your own words — not a copy. Leave out minor details. Include the main idea and the evidence that supports it.

Example
After a video about volcanoes: "The video explained that volcanoes form when magma reaches the surface. It showed three types: shield, cinder cone, and composite." Two key details + main idea.
8. Purpose of a Speaker (SL.5.3)
Author's Purpose (PIE)
Persuade · Inform · Entertain

Every speaker has a purpose. Listen for clues: opinion words + call to action = persuade; facts + definitions = inform; story + humor = entertain. A talk can have more than one purpose.

Example
A student speech about litter starts: "Did you know 8 billion pounds of plastic enter the ocean each year? We must act now!" — Purpose: inform AND persuade.
9. Point of View in Media (SL.5.3)
Identifying Point of View
Claim + Evidence → reveals POV

A speaker's point of view (perspective) shapes what they include and leave out. Listen for their claim and the evidence they choose — this reveals their position on the topic.

Example
Two podcasts about school uniforms: one interviews parents who like uniforms; the other interviews students who dislike them. Each has a different point of view on the same topic.
10. Multimedia Features (SL.5.5)
Multimedia & Meaning
Visual/audio features enhance the message

Speakers use multimedia (charts, images, video, audio, maps) to support and clarify their message. Ask: how does this feature add to what the speaker is saying?

Example
A speaker talks about rising sea levels and shows a map where coastal cities are shaded. The map helps the audience visualize the information better than words alone.
11. Formal vs. Informal Language (SL.5.6)
Register
Formal = school/work · Informal = casual talk

Good speakers adjust their register (language style) to the situation. Use formal language for presentations, discussions, and writing; informal language is fine among friends.

Example
Formal: "I would like to present my findings on ocean pollution." Informal: "So I found out the ocean is, like, really messed up." Match your register to your audience.
12. Asking Clarifying Questions (SL.5.3)
Clarifying Questions
"Could you explain…?" / "What do you mean by…?"

Clarifying questions help you understand something you missed or found confusing. A good clarifying question is polite, specific, and asked at an appropriate moment.

Example
After a classmate's report: "You mentioned 'photosynthesis energy cycle' — could you explain what that cycle looks like?" Specific + polite = good clarifying question.
🔴 Hard
13. Evaluating Evidence in a Talk (SL.5.3)
Evidence Quality Check
Strong: facts, data, experts · Weak: opinion, vague

Not all evidence is equal. Strong evidence includes statistics, expert quotes, and verified facts. Weak evidence includes personal opinions, emotional appeals, or vague claims like "everyone knows."

Example
"Studies by NASA show Arctic ice has decreased by 13% per decade" = strong. "A lot of people think the climate is changing" = weak — who? how many? Always ask: how do they know?
14. Integrating Info from Multiple Sources (SL.5.2)
Integration Strategy
Compare sources → find agreement → note differences

Strong listeners combine information from multiple sources (video, audio, text). Look for what sources agree on — that strengthens the claim. Note any contradictions and consider why they exist.

Example
A documentary and a podcast both say pandas are endangered, but disagree on the number. The agreement strengthens the main point; the disagreement is worth noting and investigating.
15. Reasoned Discussion (SL.5.1c)
Discourse Moves
Agree/disagree with reasons · Build on · Challenge politely

In academic discussions, use discourse moves: agree with evidence ("I agree because…"), respectfully disagree ("I see it differently because…"), or build on ideas ("Adding to what James said…").

Example
"I respectfully disagree with the idea that social media is always harmful. Research by Pew shows teens also use it to connect with family during crises." Disagree + evidence = reasoned response.
16. Adapting Speech to Purpose (SL.5.4)
Adaptation Factors
Audience · Purpose · Context → adjust vocabulary & pace

Skilled speakers adapt their vocabulary, examples, pace, and tone based on who is listening and why. The same content might be explained very differently to a 6-year-old vs. a scientist.

Example
Explaining gravity to kindergartners: "Heavy things fall down fast." To a science class: "Gravitational acceleration near Earth's surface is approximately 9.8 m/s²." Same fact, different register.
17. Bias in Audio/Video (SL.5.3)
Detecting Bias
One-sided? Missing voices? Loaded language?

Bias means favoring one viewpoint unfairly. In media, listen for: only one side presented, emotional or loaded words, missing opposing evidence, or who is NOT being interviewed.

Example
A news segment about a new park only interviews people who support it and uses words like "wonderful community gift." Bias clue: no opposing voices, no cost data, positive loaded language.
18. Critique a Presentation (SL.5.3)
Effective Critique
Specific praise + specific suggestion (not just "good job")

A good critique uses specific evidence: name what worked and why, then name one improvement with a concrete suggestion. Vague feedback ("nice job") is not useful. Focus on content, delivery, and language.

Example
"Your statistics about deforestation were compelling (specific praise), but your conclusion only restated the intro — adding a call to action would make it stronger (specific suggestion)."
