1. Main Idea (5.RI.2)
Main Idea Formula
Main idea = central message + most important point
The main idea is what the entire text is mostly about — not just one paragraph. It is broader than any single detail. Ask: "If I had to sum up this text in one sentence, what would I say?"
Worked Example
"A passage about bees making honey" →
Main idea: "Bees produce honey through a complex process."
(Not just "bees fly" — that's a detail)
2. Key Details & Evidence (5.RI.1)
Evidence Starters
"The text says…" or "According to the author…"
Evidence is a direct quote or specific detail from the text that proves your point. Always cite where the evidence comes from. Look for specific names, dates, numbers, and quoted speech.
Worked Example
"What evidence shows the Forest Service protected bears?"
→ Cite: "The program allows bears to fish safely away from crowds"
3. Story Theme (5.RL.2)
Theme Formula
Theme ≠ topic; Theme = life lesson from the whole story
The theme is the universal message or lesson the author wants readers to take away. The topic is what the story is about (one word or phrase); the theme is the deeper meaning (a full sentence about life).
Worked Example
Topic: "friendship"
Theme: "True friends support each other in hard times."
(Always write theme as a complete sentence)
4. Vocabulary in Context (5.RL.4)
Context Clue Strategy
Look at words BEFORE and AFTER the unknown word
Context clues are words, phrases, and sentences near the unfamiliar word that help you figure out its meaning. Look for definitions, synonyms, examples, or descriptions the author provides nearby.
Worked Example
"The barren desert had no plants." →
Clues: "no plants," "no life" → barren means empty, without life
5. Author's Purpose (5.RI.8)
Author's Purpose
PIE — Persuade, Inform, Entertain
Every text has a purpose. Informational texts share facts. Persuasive texts try to change your thinking. Entertaining texts tell stories or make you feel something. A text can have more than one purpose.
Worked Example
Recipe article → Inform (how to make something)
Editorial about pollution → Persuade (change behavior)
6. Story Elements (5.RL.3)
Story Elements
Character · Setting · Problem · Events · Solution
Every story has basic elements. Characters are the people or animals in the story. Setting is where and when it happens. The plot includes the problem, events that build tension, and the solution.
Worked Example
"In a snowy village (setting), a young girl (character) discovers her missing dog (problem) by following pawprints (event) and finds him safe (solution)."
🟡 Medium
7. Text Structure (5.RI.5)
Signal Words
"because" = cause-effect · "however" = contrast · "first/next" = sequence
Authors organize their writing using specific structures. Signal words help you identify which structure is being used. Recognizing text structure helps you understand how ideas are related.
Worked Example
"Floods cause damage because rivers overflow" → cause-effect structure
"However, e-bikes are popular" → compare-contrast
8. Character Motivation (5.RL.3)
Motivation Formula
Action + Evidence + Reason = Motivation
Character motivation explains WHY a character does what they do. Look at their actions, the text evidence that supports your thinking, and reason through what drives those actions based on emotions, goals, or circumstances.
Worked Example
"Why did Jack climb the beanstalk?"
He was curious AND wanted to help his family who was poor — text evidence: he had already traded their cow.
9. Figurative Language (5.RL.4)
Figurative Language
Simile = like/as · Metaphor = is/was · Idiom = non-literal
Figurative language adds meaning beyond the literal words. Similes compare using "like" or "as." Metaphors say one thing IS another. Idioms are phrases whose meaning differs from their literal words.
Worked Example
"Her smile was a ray of sunshine" (metaphor) →
Meaning: her smile was warm and bright (not literally sunlight)
10. Compare and Contrast (5.RI.3)
Venn Diagram Formula
Overlap = same · Outer circles = different
When comparing two texts or ideas, use a Venn diagram to organize similarities and differences. The overlap shows what both have in common. The outer sections show what is unique to each.
Worked Example
Two e-bike articles: both agree e-bikes save gas; disagree on safety → use Venn to organize comparison
11. Making Inferences (5.RI.1, 5.RL.1)
Inference Formula
Inference = text clue + what you already know
An inference is a conclusion drawn from evidence plus your background knowledge. The author doesn't directly state it, but the clues in the text lead you there. Always support inferences with text evidence.
Worked Example
"She grabbed her umbrella before leaving." →
Clue: umbrella + knowledge: umbrellas block rain
Inference: it's raining or might rain outside
12. Point of View (5.RL.5)
Point of View
1st = I/we · 3rd limited = he/she (one character) · 3rd omniscient = all characters
Point of view affects what information readers get. First person lets us inside the narrator's mind but limits what we know. Third person omniscient gives access to all characters' thoughts.
Worked Example
"I saw the sunset" = 1st person (narrator is in the story)
"She saw the sunset" = 3rd person (narrator outside the story)
🔴 Hard
13. Summarizing (5.RI.2)
Summary Formula
Who + did what + why + result (no opinions)
A summary is a short, objective retelling of the main events. Include only the most important information: who, what they did, and why. Leave out minor details, opinions, and personal reactions.
Worked Example
"Passage about Space Probes" → Summary:
"Scientists launched probes starting in 1957 to explore space and study planets."
(Not: "I think space is cool and…")
14. Text Evidence for Arguments (5.RI.8)
Evidence Argument
Claim + Direct Quote + Explanation = supported argument
Strong textual arguments use direct quotes from the text as evidence. Introduce the quote, provide it in quotation marks, and then explain how it supports your claim. Never let a quote speak for itself.
Worked Example
Claim → Evidence: "The program allows bears to fish safely away from crowds" → Explanation: This shows they planned specifically for bears' wellbeing.
15. Synthesizing Two Texts (5.RI.9)
Synthesis Formula
What do BOTH say? What does each say alone? New idea?
Synthesis goes beyond comparison — you take ideas from multiple sources and combine them to form a new, deeper understanding. Ask: what do both agree on, what is unique to each, and what new conclusion can you draw?
Worked Example
Two articles on service animals → Synthesis:
"Both suggest animals help people, but they differ on which animals are best for specific tasks."
16. Connotation & Denotation (5.L.5a)
Word Tone
Denotation = dictionary meaning · Connotation = emotional tone
Words have dictionary meanings (denotation) and emotional associations (connotation). Authors carefully choose words for their connotations to influence how readers feel about characters, events, or ideas.
Worked Example
"Slender" vs "skinny" — both mean thin.
"Slender" is positive/elegant; "skinny" can sound negative.
Authors use these to shape reader perception.
17. Structure's Effect on Meaning (5.RI.5)
Structure's Effect
Cause-effect = shows consequences · Chronological = follows events in time
The same content organized differently creates a different reading experience. Chronological order helps readers follow events. Cause-effect structure emphasizes consequences and relationships between events.
Worked Example
History essay in chronological order: background → event → impact.
Readers can follow the timeline clearly. If written cause-effect: emphasizes WHY events matter.
18. Author's Craft (5.RL.5)
Author's Craft
Identify technique → explain effect on reader
Authors use craft techniques intentionally to create effects. Foreshadowing hints at what's to come. Flashbacks provide backstory. Dialogue reveals character. Description sets mood. Always explain the effect, not just the technique.
Worked Example
Dialogue: "I'll never give up!" → Effect: shows the character is determined and resilient
(Don't just say "it's dialogue" — explain WHY the author used it)
