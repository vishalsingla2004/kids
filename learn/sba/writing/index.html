🟢 Easy
1. Capitalization (5.L.2)
Capitalize
First word · Proper nouns · "I" · Titles
Capital letters mark the start of sentences, show that something is a specific named person, place, or thing (proper noun), and are always used for the pronoun "I." Days, months, and titles are also capitalized.
Worked Example
"my friend sarah lives on maple street" →
"My friend Sarah lives on Maple Street"
2. Punctuation (5.L.2d)
Punctuation Rules
Comma: lists, FANBOYS, intro phrase · Apostrophe: contractions & possession
Commas separate items in lists, join independent clauses before FANBOYS conjunctions, and follow introductory phrases. Apostrophes show possession (Sarah's book) or mark missing letters in contractions (it's = it is).
Worked Example
"It's Sarah's book, and it belongs to her."
(contraction + possessive + comma before "and")
3. Complete Sentences
Complete Sentence
Subject (who/what) + Predicate (what they do)
A complete sentence has both a subject (who or what the sentence is about) and a predicate (what the subject does or is). A fragment is missing one of these parts. Run-on sentences join two complete sentences without proper punctuation.
Worked Example
Fragment: "The hungry cat." (no predicate)
Complete: "The hungry cat sat on the warm mat."
4. Transitions (5.W.1d)
Transition Use
Use transitions to show relationships between ideas
Transitions are words or phrases that connect ideas and show how they relate. They create flow between sentences and paragraphs. Choosing the right type shows whether you're adding, contrasting, or sequencing ideas.
Worked Example
"I love reading. However, math is my favorite subject."
("However" = contrast — showing these two ideas are different)
5. Precise Word Choice (5.W.2d)
Word Choice Rule
Replace said/went/nice/bad with vivid, specific words
Precise word choice makes writing more interesting and clearer. Replace overused words like "said," "nice," "went," and "big" with specific words that capture the exact meaning and feeling you intend.
Worked Example
"He walked slowly" → "He shuffled" or "He trudged"
(trudged = walked wearily through difficulty — much more vivid)
6. Sentence Types
Sentence Types
Simple = 1 clause · Compound = 2 independent · Complex = independent + dependent
Varying sentence types makes writing more interesting. Simple sentences are direct. Compound sentences join two equal ideas. Complex sentences show relationships using subordinating conjunctions like "although," "because," "when."
Worked Example
Complex: "Although it rained, we played outside."
("Although" = subordinating conjunction; first clause is dependent)
🟡 Medium
7. Informative Writing (5.W.2b)
Informative Structure
Topic sentence + details + examples + concluding sentence
Informative writing explains or teaches. Each body paragraph begins with a topic sentence, provides facts and details, and ends with a closing sentence. Use domain-specific vocabulary and evidence, not opinions.
Worked Example
"Bees are important pollinators. They transfer pollen between flowers, helping plants reproduce. Without bees, many crops would not grow."
8. Narrative Writing (5.W.3a)
Narrative Structure
Orient reader → complication → resolution
Narratives tell a story with a clear beginning, middle, and end. Orient the reader first (who, where, when). Build tension through the middle. Resolve the conflict at the end. Use dialogue and description for vivid storytelling.
Worked Example
Opening: "On a foggy morning, I heard an unusual sound in the backyard…"
(establishes setting, character, and hooks the reader)
9. Grammar: Verb Tense (5.L.1)
Verb Tense
Past: -ed or irregular · Perfect: has/have + past participle
Use consistent verb tense throughout a piece of writing. Past tense uses -ed for regular verbs and irregular forms (ran, ate) for others. Perfect tense shows completed actions: "has run," "have eaten." Avoid shifting tenses without reason.
Worked Example
Error: "I runned fast." Fix: "I ran fast."
Perfect: "I have run three miles every day this week."
10. Subject-Verb Agreement (5.L.1b)
Agreement Rule
She/he/it/singular → verb+s · They/we/I/plural → base verb
The subject and verb must agree in number. Singular subjects use verbs with -s (she runs). Plural subjects use the base verb (they run). This rule applies even when other words come between the subject and verb.
Worked Example
Error: "The dogs runs fast." Fix: "The dogs run fast."
(dogs = plural → base verb "run")
11. Opinion Writing (5.W.1b)
Opinion Structure
State opinion → reason → evidence → repeat × 2-3
Opinion writing argues for a point of view. State your opinion clearly first. Give 2-3 reasons. Support each reason with evidence. Use signal words: "because," "for example," "therefore." End with a strong conclusion.
Worked Example
"School should start later because students need more sleep. Studies show that teenagers need 9 hours…"
12. Domain-Specific Vocabulary (5.L.6)
Domain Vocabulary
Use precise terms from the subject you're writing about
Domain-specific vocabulary shows expertise and communicates more precisely. In science writing, use "precipitation" instead of "water stuff from clouds." In math, use "perpendicular" instead of "straight up-down line."
Worked Example
Vague: "the water stuff in clouds"
Precise: "precipitation" or "condensation"
(domain vocabulary = more precise and professional)
🔴 Hard
13. Strong Conclusions (5.W.1d, 5.W.2e)
Conclusion Formula
Don't just repeat — synthesize or extend the idea
A strong conclusion does more than restate the intro. It synthesizes the argument, makes a broader statement, issues a call to action, or looks to the future. Leave the reader with a lasting impression.
Worked Example
Weak: "So those are my three reasons."
Strong: "Changing school start times would help every student perform better and live healthier."
14. Logical Organization (5.W.4)
Organization Rule
Order = logical progression (cause before effect)
Paragraphs should follow a logical order where each idea builds on the previous one. For history: background before event, event before impact. For arguments: weakest to strongest reason. Disorganized writing confuses readers.
Worked Example
History essay: background → event → impact ✓
NOT: impact → background → event ✗
Order must mirror how things actually happened or logically connect.
15. Citing Evidence from Text (5.W.8)
Quote Integration
Introduce + "Direct quote" + Explain how it supports your point
Never drop a quote into your writing without introduction and explanation. Introduce who said it or where it came from. Provide the quote in quotation marks. Then explain how it proves your point.
Worked Example
"According to the article, 'cement mixed with rocks forms concrete' (source). This shows that concrete requires combining two materials."
16. Argumentative Writing (5.W.1)
Argument Structure
Claim → evidence → address counterclaim → reinforce claim
Strong argumentative writing acknowledges the opposing view (counterclaim) and then refutes it. This shows you have considered all sides and makes your argument more credible and convincing.
Worked Example
"Some say e-bikes are dangerous, but studies show riders wear helmets more often than regular cyclists…"
(counterclaim acknowledged + rebutted)
17. Formal vs. Informal Style (5.L.3)
Academic Writing
Avoid contractions, slang, and second person "you"
Academic writing uses formal language. Avoid contractions (use "it is" not "it's"), slang ("cause" → "because"), and second person address ("you should" → "one should" or rephrase). Write as if addressing a professional audience.
Worked Example
Informal: "You shouldn't litter cause it's bad."
Formal: "Littering damages ecosystems and should be prohibited."
18. Revising for Clarity (5.W.5)
Revision Checklist
Is each sentence clear? Ideas in order? Evidence used?
Revision means improving writing, not just fixing spelling. Check that every sentence is clear and specific. Make sure ideas flow logically. Replace vague words with precise ones. Add or cut details as needed for clarity and strength.
Worked Example
Weak: "It was good."
Revised: "The program successfully protected both bears and visitors by separating their activities."
