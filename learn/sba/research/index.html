1. Research Question (W.5.7)
Good Research Question
Focused · Answerable · Interesting · Researchable

A good research question is specific enough to answer in a project but important enough to be worth researching. Avoid questions answerable with yes/no or a single fact.

Example
Too broad: "What is pollution?" Better: "How does plastic pollution affect sea turtle populations in the Pacific?" Specific topic + specific angle = researchable question.
2. Types of Sources (W.5.8)
Source Types
Primary = firsthand · Secondary = analysis

Primary sources are original (diaries, interviews, photographs, data). Secondary sources analyze or describe primary sources (textbooks, encyclopedia articles). Both are valuable for research.

Example
Researching MLK Jr.: his "I Have a Dream" speech = primary source. A textbook chapter about the Civil Rights Movement = secondary source.
3. Reliable vs. Unreliable Sources (W.5.8)
CRAAP Test
Currency · Relevance · Authority · Accuracy · Purpose

Use the CRAAP test to evaluate sources. Ask: Is it recent? Does it match your topic? Is the author qualified? Are facts supported? Does it inform or try to sell something?

Example
A 2009 website with no author selling a health product fails Authority, Currency, and Purpose tests. A NASA.gov article from 2023 passes all five. Always prefer credible, current, expert sources.
4. Taking Notes (W.5.8)
Note-Taking Rules
Key facts only · Your own words · Record the source

Good notes capture key facts in your own words (paraphrase) and always include the source so you can cite it later. Avoid copying entire sentences — that leads to unintentional plagiarism.

Example
Source: "Blue whales can produce sounds louder than a jet engine." Note: "Blue whale sounds = louder than jet (NatGeo, p. 22)." Paraphrase + source = good note.
5. Paraphrase vs. Plagiarism (W.5.8)
Paraphrase = Own Words + Source
Change structure AND vocabulary · Cite the source

Plagiarism is copying someone's words without credit. Paraphrasing means restating an idea in completely new wording AND citing the source. Changing only a few words is still plagiarism.

Example
Original: "Rainforests cover only 6% of Earth's surface." Plagiarism: same sentence. Paraphrase: "Although rainforests take up just 6% of our planet, they play a vital role in global climate (Smith, 2022)."
6. Using Quotations (W.5.8)
Quote Sandwich
Introduce → Quote → Explain

Direct quotations use the author's exact words in quotation marks. Always introduce the quote, include it in quotes, then explain how it supports your point. Never drop a quote in without context.

Example
Introduce: "Scientist Jane Goodall explains," Quote: "Chimpanzees show empathy much like humans." Explain: "This shows that complex emotions are not unique to people."
🟡 Medium
7. Organizing Research (W.5.7)
Research Organization
Question → Subtopics → Sources per subtopic

Break your research question into subtopics. Gather at least 2 sources per subtopic. Group your notes by subtopic so writing flows logically from one idea to the next.

Example
Research Q: "How does ocean pollution affect marine life?" Subtopics: (1) plastic effects on fish, (2) chemical runoff, (3) solutions. Each subtopic gets its own set of sources and notes.
8. Comparing Sources (W.5.9)
Source Comparison
Agree on facts = strong claim · Disagree = investigate

When multiple sources agree on a fact, that fact is more reliable. When sources disagree, look for a third source to resolve the conflict or acknowledge the disagreement in your writing.

Example
Source A and B both say rainforests hold 50% of species (agree → use it confidently). They disagree on deforestation rate: look for a third, more current source to resolve it.
9. Drawing Conclusions (RI.5.9)
Conclusion = Inference
What do ALL the evidence pieces suggest together?

A research conclusion is a logical inference drawn from multiple pieces of evidence. It goes beyond restating facts — it synthesizes them to answer your research question.

Example
Evidence: coral bleaching rising, ocean temps rising, CO₂ levels rising. Conclusion: "Human-caused climate change is the primary driver of coral reef decline." — Synthesizes all three pieces.
10. Citations & Bibliography (W.5.8)
Why Cite?
Credit authors · Avoid plagiarism · Let readers verify

Citations give credit to the original author, prevent plagiarism, and allow readers to find your sources. A bibliography (or Works Cited) lists all sources used at the end of a project.

Example
Book citation: Smith, Jane. Oceans in Danger. National Geographic Press, 2022. Website: "Coral Reefs." NOAA.gov, 15 Jan. 2024. Always alphabetize by author's last name.
11. Fact vs. Opinion (RI.5.8)
Clue Words for Opinion
Best · Worst · Should · Believe · Think · Feel

A fact can be verified with evidence. An opinion expresses a judgment or belief. In research, use facts as evidence; recognize when sources state opinions rather than facts.

Example
Fact: "Deforestation decreased by 12% in 2022." Opinion: "We must stop deforestation immediately." The opinion might be supported by facts, but it is still a judgment — not a fact itself.
12. Logical Fallacies (RI.5.8)
Common Fallacies
Ad hominem · Bandwagon · False cause · Either/Or

A logical fallacy is a flaw in reasoning. Recognizing fallacies helps you evaluate arguments critically. Sources that use fallacies instead of evidence are less trustworthy.

Example
A commercial: "9 out of 10 kids prefer our juice!" (bandwagon). Ask: What's the sample size? Who conducted the study? Paid by the company? Question the reasoning behind every claim.
🔴 Hard
13. Synthesizing Multiple Sources (W.5.9)
Synthesis ≠ Summary
Weave sources together to create a new insight

Synthesis goes beyond summarizing each source separately. You weave information from multiple sources together to build a new, unified understanding. Sources should talk to each other in your writing.

Example
Instead of: "Source A says X. Source B says Y." Write: "Both Source A and B confirm that deforestation accelerates climate change; however, Source B adds that local communities are also disproportionately harmed (Source B, p.12)."
14. Evaluating Argument Strength (RI.5.8)
Strong Argument = C + E + R
Clear Claim + Credible Evidence + Sound Reasoning

A strong argument has: (1) a clear claim, (2) credible, relevant evidence, (3) logical reasoning connecting them, and ideally addresses the counterargument. Check all four.

Example
Weak: "Recess is important because kids like it." (no evidence, vague) Strong: "30 minutes of recess improves focus by 20% according to a 2021 CDC study, demonstrating that physical breaks enhance learning." C + E + R ✓
15. Research Report Structure (W.5.7)
Report Blueprint
Intro (thesis) → Body (subtopics + evidence) → Conclusion

A research report has a clear thesis (your answer to the research question) in the intro, body paragraphs organized by subtopic each with evidence, and a conclusion that explains the broader significance.

Example
Thesis: "Ocean plastic pollution is the greatest threat to marine ecosystems." Body P1: effects on fish. Body P2: effects on birds. Body P3: effects on food chains. Conclusion: why action is urgent.
16. Counterarguments in Research (W.5.1)
Counterclaim Structure
Acknowledge opponent's view → Rebut with evidence

Strong research acknowledges counterarguments — opposing viewpoints. By addressing them and providing a rebuttal with evidence, you show intellectual honesty and strengthen your own position.

Example
"Some argue that zoos are harmful to animals. However, the World Wildlife Fund reports that 800 species have been saved from extinction through zoo breeding programs, outweighing the concern." — Acknowledge + Rebuttal with evidence.
17. Digital Literacy (W.5.8)
URL Trust Signals
.gov/.edu = usually trustworthy · .com = verify carefully

Not all websites are equally reliable. Look for: known author with credentials, publication date, cited sources, and a domain that suggests authority (.gov, .edu). Cross-check claims with a second credible source.

Example
"climatetruth.xyz" with no author, flashy ads, and no citations = red flags. "climate.nasa.gov" with credited scientists and cited data = trustworthy. Always cross-reference with a second reliable source.
18. Research Presentation (SL.5.4 + W.5.7)
Research Culminating Task
Write + present findings with evidence and citations

Research projects often end in both a written report and an oral presentation. Apply all research skills: credible sources, paraphrase + quotation, synthesis, logical argument, proper citations, and adapted delivery.

Example
Final SBA-style task: "Using at least 3 sources, write a 5-paragraph report on climate change and present your findings to the class, including one chart or diagram." — Tests all research and communication skills simultaneously.
