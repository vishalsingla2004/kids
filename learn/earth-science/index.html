Easy — Core Concepts

6 cards

🌐 Earth's Layers EASY

Crust:Outermost solid layer, 5–70 km thick. Oceanic crust is thinner and denser; continental crust is thicker.
Mantle:Thickest layer (2,870 km). Semi-solid rock that flows slowly. Convection currents here drive plate movement.
Outer Core:Liquid iron and nickel. Its movement generates Earth's magnetic field.
Inner Core:Solid iron and nickel. Hottest part (~5,000°C). Solid due to intense pressure despite extreme heat.

Earth formed about 4.6 billion years ago. Denser materials sank to the centre (iron/nickel) while lighter rock rose to form the crust — a process called differentiation. We know about Earth's interior from how seismic (earthquake) waves travel through it.

Remember: Crust → Mantle → Outer Core → Inner Core. The mantle makes up about 84% of Earth's volume. Mantle convection is the engine behind all plate tectonic activity.

🪨 Rock Types & the Rock Cycle EASY

Igneous:Formed when magma (underground) or lava (surface) cools & solidifies. Intrusive: cools slowly underground → large crystals (e.g. granite). Extrusive: cools fast at surface → small crystals (e.g. basalt).
Sedimentary:Layers of sediment (sand, silt, shells) compressed over millions of years. Examples: limestone, sandstone, chalk. Can contain fossils.
Metamorphic:Existing rocks transformed by heat and/or pressure. Limestone → marble; shale → slate; sandstone → quartzite.

All three rock types can be converted into one another through the rock cycle. This cycle is driven by plate tectonics, the water cycle, and Earth's internal heat. No rock is permanently one type — given enough time and the right conditions, any rock can change.

Key fact: Fossils are only found in sedimentary rock because the other types involve conditions (extreme heat/pressure/melting) that would destroy organic material. Most of Earth's surface rock is sedimentary, but the most abundant rock by volume in the crust is igneous.

💧 The Water Cycle EASY

Evaporation:Sun's energy heats surface water → turns to water vapour → rises into atmosphere. Also: transpiration from plants adds vapour.
Condensation:Water vapour rises, cools, and condenses around tiny dust particles → forms clouds and fog.
Precipitation:Water falls from clouds as rain, snow, sleet, or hail depending on temperature.
Collection:Water collects in oceans, lakes, rivers (surface runoff) or soaks into soil (groundwater). Eventually returns to step 1.

The water cycle is a closed system — Earth's total water stays the same, it just changes location and state (solid, liquid, gas). The cycle is powered by solar energy and gravity. It distributes fresh water across the planet and regulates temperature.

Key numbers: About 97% of Earth's water is in the oceans (salt water). Of the 3% fresh water, most (~69%) is locked in glaciers and ice caps. Only about 0.3% of all fresh water is in rivers and lakes — what most life depends on.

🌬️ Atmosphere Layers EASY

Troposphere:All weather (clouds, rain, storms) occurs here. Contains ~75% of atmospheric mass. Temperature drops ~6.5°C per km.
Stratosphere:Ozone (O₃) layer absorbs harmful UV radiation. Temperature increases upward (unique). Passenger jets cruise here.
Mesosphere:Coldest layer. Most meteors (shooting stars) burn up here through friction with air molecules.
Thermosphere:Aurora borealis/australis visible here. Extremely hot (~2000°C) but very sparse air — you'd still freeze.
Exosphere:Outermost layer. Gradually merges with outer space. Satellites orbit here.

Temperature does not change uniformly through the atmosphere — it alternates between decreasing and increasing with altitude. This is because different layers are heated by different mechanisms (ground radiation vs. ozone absorption vs. solar wind interactions).

Key fact: The ozone layer in the stratosphere is vital for life on Earth — it absorbs 97–99% of the Sun's harmful UV radiation. CFCs (chlorofluorocarbons) from aerosols damaged it, leading to international agreements (Montreal Protocol, 1987) that helped it recover.

🌦️ Weather vs Climate EASY

Weather:Short-term atmospheric conditions at a specific place and time. Can change hour by hour. Examples: today's temperature, rain, wind.
Climate:The average weather patterns over 30+ years for a region. Changes very slowly. Example: "London has a temperate maritime climate."
Climate zones:Polar (very cold, ice), Temperate (4 seasons, moderate), Tropical (hot and wet year-round near equator).
Greenhouse effect:CO₂, methane, and water vapour trap heat from escaping Earth → warms the planet. Natural process, but enhanced by human emissions.

The classic distinction: "Climate is what you expect, weather is what you get." A region's climate is determined by latitude, altitude, distance from ocean, and ocean currents. Climate change is the long-term shift in these averages due to increased greenhouse gases since industrialisation.

Example: Seattle has a reputation for rain (climate). But any given day in Seattle might be sunny (weather). — The UK's climate is warmed significantly by the Gulf Stream ocean current, making it much milder than its latitude would suggest.

⚡ Renewable vs Non-Renewable Energy EASY

Fossil fuels:Coal, oil, natural gas — formed from ancient organisms over millions of years. Finite. Burning releases CO₂ and other greenhouse gases.
Solar:Photovoltaic (PV) cells convert sunlight to electricity. Abundant and free — but depends on sunlight hours.
Wind:Turbines convert kinetic energy of wind. No emissions, but land-use concerns and not always windy.
Hydroelectric:Flowing/falling water drives turbines. Reliable and large-scale but requires rivers/dams.
Geothermal:Heat from inside Earth heats water to steam → drives turbines. Reliable and low-emission.
Biomass:Burning organic material (wood, crops). Carbon-neutral if managed sustainably.

Fossil fuels currently provide ~80% of the world's energy but are responsible for most human-caused CO₂ emissions. The transition to renewable energy is essential to prevent the worst effects of climate change. Energy storage (batteries) is a key challenge for solar and wind power.

Key comparison: In 2023, the UK generated more electricity from renewables (wind, solar, hydro, biomass: ~50%) than from fossil fuels for the first time since the Industrial Revolution. Global renewable capacity is growing faster than any energy source in history.

🗺️ Plate Tectonics MEDIUM

Convergent:Two plates collide. If oceanic meets continental → subduction (oceanic plate sinks) → volcanoes, deep trenches. Two continental plates → fold mountains (e.g. Himalayas).
Divergent:Plates pull apart → magma rises and fills gap → new crust forms (seafloor spreading). Creates mid-ocean ridges (e.g. Mid-Atlantic Ridge).
Transform:Plates slide horizontally past each other → no crust created or destroyed → major earthquakes. Example: San Andreas Fault (California).
Subduction:When one plate sinks beneath another into the mantle. Creates the deepest ocean trenches and volcanic arcs.

Earth's lithosphere (crust + upper mantle) is broken into about 15 major tectonic plates that move 2–10 cm per year — roughly the speed your fingernails grow. This movement is driven by convection currents in the mantle. Over millions of years, plates have assembled and broken apart "supercontinents" like Pangaea (~250 mya).

Ring of Fire: A 40,000 km horseshoe-shaped zone around the Pacific Ocean containing 75% of the world's volcanoes and 90% of earthquakes. Caused by multiple convergent plate boundaries where oceanic plates subduct under continental plates.

🌋 Volcanoes & Earthquakes MEDIUM

Volcano types:Shield (gentle slopes, fluid lava — Hawaii), Stratovolcano/composite (steep, explosive — Mt Fuji, Mt St Helens), Cinder cone (small, steep, brief eruptions).
Focus vs Epicenter:The focus (hypocenter) is where the earthquake originates underground. The epicenter is the point on the surface directly above — where shaking is usually strongest.
Seismic waves:P-waves (primary) — fastest, travel through solids and liquids. S-waves (secondary) — slower, only through solids. Surface waves — cause most damage.
Hotspots:Volcanic activity NOT at plate boundaries — caused by a plume of hot mantle rock. Hawaii and Yellowstone are hotspot volcanoes.

Earthquakes and volcanoes are closely linked to plate boundaries but work differently. Earthquakes result from sudden release of stress when rocks fracture along faults. Volcanoes form where magma can reach the surface — either at divergent boundaries, subduction zones, or hotspots. Scientists cannot yet predict earthquakes precisely.

Key fact: The 2004 Indian Ocean earthquake (magnitude 9.1) released energy equivalent to 23,000 Hiroshima atomic bombs. It triggered a tsunami that killed ~230,000 people across 14 countries — one of the deadliest natural disasters in recorded history.

🏔️ Erosion & Deposition MEDIUM

Weathering:Breaking down of rock in place. Physical: freeze-thaw, temperature changes crack rock. Chemical: acid rain dissolves limestone (e.g. caves). Biological: plant roots split rock.
Erosion:Wearing away and removal of material by water, wind, ice, or gravity. Happens faster on steep slopes and with heavier rainfall.
Transportation:Eroded material carried downstream. Particles collide with each other → become rounded and smaller (attrition).
Deposition:When energy drops (river slows), material is dropped. Heaviest particles drop first. Creates deltas, flood plains, beaches, and sand dunes.

Erosion and deposition work together to sculpt Earth's landscapes over millions of years. The Grand Canyon was carved by the Colorado River over ~6 million years. Glaciers carved the fjords of Norway. Wind erosion created the sandstone arches of Utah. Human activity (deforestation, construction) dramatically speeds up erosion.

Landforms created: River erosion → V-shaped valleys, waterfalls, gorges. River deposition → deltas, flood plains, meanders. Glacial erosion → U-shaped valleys, cirques, arêtes. Coastal erosion → cliffs, sea stacks, arches. Coastal deposition → beaches, spits, bars.

🌊 Ocean Structure MEDIUM

Sunlight zone:0–200 m. Photic zone — enough light for photosynthesis. Contains 90% of ocean life. Includes coral reefs, kelp forests, most fish.
Twilight zone:200–1,000 m. Some light but not enough for photosynthesis. Many creatures migrate here daily to feed, then return to depths. Bioluminescence common.
Midnight zone:1,000–4,000 m. Completely dark, cold (~4°C), high pressure. Strange adaptations: anglerfish lures, transparent bodies.
Abyssal zone:4,000–6,000 m. Covers ~60% of Earth's surface. Home to hydrothermal vents supporting unique ecosystems via chemosynthesis (not photosynthesis).
Ocean currents:Large-scale circulation of ocean water driven by wind, density differences (temperature & salinity). The Gulf Stream carries warm tropical water northward, warming Western Europe.

The ocean covers 71% of Earth's surface and averages 3,688 m depth. The deepest point is the Mariana Trench at ~11,000 m (deeper than Mount Everest is tall). Despite harsh conditions in deeper zones, life exists everywhere — even in the hadal zone at the bottom of ocean trenches.

Thermohaline circulation: A global "conveyor belt" of ocean currents driven by differences in water temperature (thermo) and salinity (haline). This global ocean circulation redistributes heat around the planet and is crucial for regulating climate. Climate change could slow or disrupt it with serious consequences for Europe's temperature.

🦕 Earth's History & Fossil Record MEDIUM

Earth's age:4.6 billion years (4,600,000,000). Put in perspective: if Earth's history = 1 year, humans appear in the last 4 seconds of Dec 31st.
Fossil record:Preserved remains or traces (footprints, burrows) of ancient organisms. Found almost exclusively in sedimentary rock. Shows how life has evolved over time.
Index fossils:Fossils of organisms that lived for a short, well-defined time period and were widespread. Used to date rock layers (e.g. ammonites, trilobites).
Mass extinctions:5 major events. The end-Cretaceous (65 mya) was likely caused by an asteroid impact + volcanic activity. Wiped out ~75% of all species including non-avian dinosaurs.

The geological time scale divides Earth's history into eons, eras, periods, and epochs based on changes in rock layers and fossil records. The Precambrian (4.6 billion–541 million years ago) covers 88% of Earth's history. The Phanerozoic (541 mya–present) is when complex life flourished. Scientists use radiometric dating to determine the age of rocks and fossils by measuring radioactive isotope decay.

Birds are dinosaurs: Modern birds evolved directly from theropod dinosaurs. Archaeopteryx (150 mya) shows features of both dinosaurs and birds. So technically, dinosaurs did NOT go fully extinct 65 million years ago — about 10,000 species of feathered dinosaurs are alive today.

🪐 The Solar System MEDIUM

Rocky planets:Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars — small, solid surfaces, close to Sun, relatively few moons.
Gas/ice giants:Jupiter, Saturn (gas giants — mainly hydrogen/helium). Uranus, Neptune (ice giants — water, methane ice + gas). All have ring systems; Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune have multiple moons.
Earth's motion:Rotation (spin on axis, 24h) → day/night. Revolution (orbit around Sun, 365.25 days) → 1 year. Axial tilt of 23.5° → seasons.
Moon's effects:The Moon's gravity causes Earth's ocean tides. The Moon also stabilises Earth's axial tilt, which keeps our climate stable.

Our solar system formed ~4.6 billion years ago from a cloud of gas and dust (solar nebula). The Sun contains 99.86% of the solar system's mass. Jupiter is so large that 1,300 Earths could fit inside it. Light from the Sun takes 8 minutes to reach Earth, but ~4 years to reach the nearest other star (Proxima Centauri).

Scale check: If the Sun were the size of a basketball, Earth would be the size of a sesame seed about 26 meters away. Neptune would be the size of a pea nearly 800 meters away. The nearest star system (Alpha Centauri) would be ~6,700 km away on this same scale.

♻️ The Carbon Cycle HARD

Carbon stores:Atmosphere (CO₂, CH₄), Oceans (dissolved CO₂, shells), Living things (organic molecules), Soil (humus), Fossil fuels (ancient buried carbon), Rocks (limestone — largest store).
Photosynthesis:6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂. Plants absorb CO₂ from atmosphere → remove carbon. Forests and oceans are key carbon sinks.
Respiration:C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + energy. All living things release CO₂ back. Decomposition by bacteria/fungi also releases CO₂.
Human impact:Burning fossil fuels releases carbon stored for 300+ million years into the atmosphere in decades → atmospheric CO₂ rising from 280 ppm (pre-industrial) to 420+ ppm today.

The natural carbon cycle is roughly balanced — what plants absorb, decomposition and respiration release. Human activity has broken this balance by adding ~10 billion tonnes of extra CO₂ per year from fossil fuel combustion, deforestation, and cement production. The ocean absorbs about 25% of this, causing ocean acidification (dissolving shells of marine organisms).

Feedback loops: Warming → Arctic permafrost melts → releases methane (CH₄, 80× more potent than CO₂ over 20 years) → more warming. This is a positive feedback loop (amplifies change). Methane from livestock and rice paddies also contributes significantly to greenhouse gas levels.

📈 Climate Change Evidence HARD

Scientific consensus:97%+ of climate scientists agree that current climate change is primarily caused by human activity (burning fossil fuels, deforestation, agriculture).
CO₂ levels:Pre-industrial (1750): ~280 ppm. Today (2025): ~425 ppm. Highest in at least 800,000 years (from ice core data). Rate of increase is ~100× faster than natural changes.
Ocean acidification:Oceans absorb CO₂ → forms carbonic acid (H₂CO₃) → lowers pH → dissolves calcium carbonate shells → threatens coral reefs, shellfish, marine food chains.
IPCC:Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change — UN body that synthesises thousands of scientific studies. Reports guide international climate policy.

Climate change is distinguished from natural climate variability by its speed and cause. Natural climate cycles (Milankovitch cycles, volcanic eruptions) operate over thousands to millions of years. Current changes are occurring over decades. The "fingerprint" of human-caused warming — including cooling stratosphere, warming troposphere, and nighttime temperatures rising faster than daytime — cannot be explained by natural factors alone.

The Paris Agreement (2015): 196 countries agreed to limit warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels and no more than 2°C. At current trajectories, we are on track for 2.5–3°C by 2100 without more ambitious action. Consequences: entire island nations submerged, extreme heat making parts of tropics uninhabitable, mass migration.

🌱 Soil Formation & Composition HARD

Soil formation:Pedogenesis — takes thousands of years. Starts with physical/chemical weathering of bedrock → parent material. Add organic matter from organisms → slowly becomes soil. Climate, organisms, topography, time all influence soil type.
Soil texture:Determined by particle size. Sandy (large particles, drains fast, poor nutrients), Clay (tiny particles, retains water, can waterlog), Silt (medium), Loam (mix of all three — ideal for agriculture).
Soil pH:Affects which nutrients plants can absorb. Most crops prefer slightly acidic to neutral (pH 6–7). Acid rain, fertilisers, and industrial pollution can alter soil pH, reducing fertility.
Threats to soil:Erosion (wind/water removes topsoil), compaction (heavy machinery reduces air spaces), salinisation (irrigation causes salt buildup), contamination (heavy metals, pesticides).

Soil is a complex ecosystem — a single teaspoon contains more microorganisms than there are people on Earth. These bacteria, fungi, and other organisms drive nutrient cycles essential for plant growth. Earthworms aerate and mix soil horizons. Soil is essentially a non-renewable resource on human timescales — one of the most precious and underappreciated on the planet.

Food security: 95% of our food is directly or indirectly produced in soil. Globally, about one-third of soil is degraded, and we are losing productive topsoil 10–40× faster than it forms. Sustainable farming practices (cover crops, reduced tillage, composting) are essential to maintain soil health for future food production.

🌍 Seasons & Earth's Axial Tilt HARD

Why seasons occur:Earth's axis is tilted at 23.5° relative to its orbital plane. As Earth orbits the Sun, each hemisphere alternates tilting toward and away from the Sun.
Summer (N. hemisphere):Northern hemisphere tilts toward Sun → more direct sunlight (higher angle = more energy per m²) → longer days. June 21 = summer solstice (longest day).
Winter (N. hemisphere):Northern hemisphere tilts away from Sun → sunlight hits at low angle → less energy per m² → shorter days. Dec 21 = winter solstice (shortest day).
Equinoxes:Twice a year (March 20 & Sept 22), neither hemisphere tilts toward Sun → 12 hours of daylight everywhere on Earth. "Equinox" = "equal night".
At the equator:The equator always receives relatively direct sunlight → no strong seasons. Poles experience extreme variation: 24-hour daylight in summer, 24-hour darkness in winter.

Two effects of tilt cause seasons: (1) Angle of sunlight — when sunlight strikes at a lower angle, the same amount of energy is spread over a larger area → less intense heating. (2) Day length — more hours of daylight = more total energy received. Both effects reinforce each other to produce seasons. Distance from the Sun is NOT the cause — Earth is actually closest to the Sun (perihelion) in early January.

Milankovitch cycles: Over thousands to hundreds of thousands of years, three orbital parameters change: (1) eccentricity (shape of orbit), (2) axial tilt (changes between 22.1° and 24.5°), (3) precession (wobble of axis). These cycles are responsible for Earth's ice ages, occurring roughly every 100,000 years.

⭐ Stars & the Hertzsprung-Russell Diagram HARD

H-R diagram:Plots stars by luminosity (y-axis) vs surface temperature (x-axis). About 90% of stars fall on the main sequence (diagonal band) including our Sun. Stars on the main sequence fuse hydrogen → helium in their cores.
Our Sun:G-type yellow dwarf. Age: ~4.6 billion years. Estimated 5 billion years left on main sequence. Will expand into a red giant, then shed outer layers → planetary nebula → white dwarf.
Supernova:Catastrophic explosion of a massive star. For a brief moment, outshines an entire galaxy. Creates and disperses heavy elements (iron, gold, uranium) — all atoms heavier than iron were forged in supernovae.
Nuclear fusion:Stars shine by fusing hydrogen nuclei into helium at their cores (requires ~15 million °C). The energy released (E=mc²) provides outward pressure balancing gravity — this is hydrostatic equilibrium.

The H-R diagram is one of the most important tools in astrophysics — it reveals patterns in stellar evolution. A star's position on the diagram is determined by its mass. More massive stars are hotter, bluer, and more luminous, but burn through their fuel far faster. A star 10× the Sun's mass burns its fuel ~1,000× faster and lives only ~10 million years vs. the Sun's ~10 billion years.

"We are stardust": Every element in your body heavier than hydrogen and helium was forged inside a star or during a supernova explosion. The calcium in your bones, the iron in your blood, the oxygen you breathe — all were created in stellar nucleosynthesis and scattered by stellar winds and supernovae before being incorporated into our solar system.

🌿 Human Impact on the Environment HARD

Deforestation:Tropical rainforests cover 6% of land but contain 50% of species. Brazil, Indonesia, DR Congo are losing forests fastest. Causes: agriculture (soy, palm oil, cattle), logging, urban expansion.
Trophic cascades:Removing a keystone species causes cascading effects through the food web. Example: removing wolves from Yellowstone → deer overgrazing → riverbank erosion → changed river courses. Reintroducing wolves reversed this.
Bioaccumulation:Toxins (e.g. mercury, PCBs, DDT) accumulate in body fat and become more concentrated at each trophic level (biomagnification). Top predators (tuna, eagles, polar bears, humans) accumulate highest doses.
Circular economy:Moving away from "take-make-dispose" (linear) to a system where materials are kept in use as long as possible through reuse, repair, recycling, and regeneration of natural systems.

Human activity has transformed ~75% of Earth's ice-free land surface. We are in what scientists call the Anthropocene — a proposed geological epoch defined by dominant human influence on Earth's geology and ecosystems. The Planetary Boundaries framework identifies 9 Earth-system processes; we have crossed safe limits in 6, including climate change, biodiversity loss, and freshwater use.

Positive signs: The ozone hole is healing (thanks to banning CFCs). Some species have recovered: gray wolves, bald eagles, humpback whales. Renewable energy capacity is growing exponentially. Over 15% of Earth's land and 8% of oceans are now protected. Action works — but speed and scale need to increase dramatically.
