🔬 Cell Structure

Both Cells Have
Nucleus (control centre, contains DNA) · Cell membrane (controls entry/exit) · Cytoplasm (jelly-like fluid where reactions happen) · Mitochondria (make energy via respiration)
Plant Cells Also Have
Cell wall (rigid, made of cellulose — gives shape) · Chloroplasts (contain chlorophyll, site of photosynthesis) · Large permanent vacuole (stores water, keeps cell firm/turgid)

🌿 Photosynthesis

6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
  • Happens in chloroplasts — contain green pigment chlorophyll that absorbs light
  • Inputs: carbon dioxide (from air via stomata) + water (from roots via xylem) + light energy
  • Outputs: glucose (stored as starch, used for energy/growth) + oxygen (released into air)
Why leaves are green: Chlorophyll absorbs red and blue light, reflects green light back to our eyes. Variegated (white) parts of leaves cannot photosynthesise — no chlorophyll.

💨 Respiration

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP energy
  • Aerobic respiration — uses oxygen, happens in mitochondria, releases lots of energy as ATP
  • ALL living things respire — plants, animals, fungi, bacteria — to release energy from food
  • Anaerobic respiration — no oxygen needed; animals produce lactic acid (muscle fatigue); yeast produces ethanol + CO₂ (fermentation)
  • Respiration ≠ breathing. Breathing moves air in/out; respiration is a chemical process in cells
Yeast fermentation: glucose → ethanol + CO₂. Used in bread making (CO₂ makes dough rise) and brewing (ethanol makes alcohol).

🌾 Food Chains and Webs

  • Producer — makes own food via photosynthesis (plants, algae)
  • Primary consumer — herbivore; eats producers
  • Secondary consumer — eats primary consumers
  • Apex predator — top of chain; nothing hunts it
  • Arrows show direction of energy flow (what is eaten → what eats it)
  • Only ~10% of energy transfers between trophic levels; rest lost as heat/waste
Food web = many food chains linked together. More connections = more stable ecosystem. Remove one species and others are affected (knock-on effect).

📋 Classification of Living Things

  • 7 levels from broadest to most specific: Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species
  • Mnemonic: "King Philip Came Over For Good Soup"
  • Species = organisms that can interbreed and produce fertile offspring
  • Scientific name = Genus + species (e.g. Homo sapiens). Always italicised
5 Kingdoms: Animals · Plants · Fungi · Protists · Bacteria (Prokaryotes). The more classification levels two organisms share, the more closely related they are.

🐟 Vertebrates vs Invertebrates

  • Vertebrates — have a vertebral column (backbone/spine); internal skeleton (endoskeleton)
  • Invertebrates — no backbone; may have exoskeleton (insects), hydrostatic skeleton (worms), or none
  • Mammals: warm-blooded, breathe air, give birth to live young (mostly), nurse with milk, have hair/fur
  • Insects: 6 legs, 3 body segments (head, thorax, abdomen), exoskeleton, mostly 2 pairs of wings
Surprising fact: Invertebrates make up about 95% of all animal species. Insects alone account for over 1 million described species — more than all other animals combined!
🟡 Medium

🧬 Genetics — DNA and Chromosomes

  • DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) — double helix molecule carrying genetic instructions
  • Gene — a section of DNA that codes for one protein / one trait
  • Chromosome — tightly coiled DNA; humans have 46 (23 pairs) in every body cell nucleus
  • Chromosome 23 = sex chromosomes: XX = female, XY = male
  • DNA bases pair: A-T and C-G (complementary base pairing)
The Human Genome contains about 3 billion base pairs and ~20,000 genes — yet only ~2% of DNA actually codes for proteins. The rest was once called "junk DNA" but has regulatory roles.

🔁 Inheritance

  • Alleles — different versions of the same gene (e.g. R = red, r = white)
  • Dominant allele (capital letter) — expressed even if only one copy present
  • Recessive allele (lowercase) — only expressed if two copies present (rr)
  • Homozygous = two same alleles (RR or rr). Heterozygous = two different alleles (Rr)
  • Genotype = allele combination. Phenotype = physical appearance/trait expressed
Codominance: Both alleles expressed equally. E.g. red (RR) × white (WW) → pink (RW). Neither allele is fully dominant — both show in the phenotype.

🫀 Human Body Systems

  • Circulatory — double circulation: pulmonary (heart→lungs→heart) + systemic (heart→body→heart)
  • Digestive — enzymes break down food: amylase (starch), protease (protein), lipase (fat)
  • Respiratory — alveoli in lungs provide huge surface area for gas exchange by diffusion
  • Nervous — CNS (brain + spinal cord) + PNS (all other nerves)

🦠 Microorganisms

  • Bacteria — prokaryotes (no nucleus); reproduce rapidly by binary fission; some cause disease (Salmonella, TB), some are beneficial (gut bacteria, yogurt, nitrogen fixation)
  • Viruses — not living cells; protein coat + genetic material; hijack host cells to reproduce; treated by vaccines, not antibiotics
  • Fungi — eukaryotes; absorb nutrients from dead material (decomposers); some cause athlete's foot, thrush
  • Protists — single-celled eukaryotes; Plasmodium causes malaria (spread by Anopheles mosquito)
Antibiotics vs antivirals: Antibiotics kill bacteria (e.g. penicillin). They do NOT work on viruses. Overusing antibiotics leads to antibiotic-resistant bacteria (e.g. MRSA).

🦒 Evolution and Natural Selection

  • Variation — individuals in a population are not identical (genetic variation from mutations and sexual reproduction)
  • Selection pressure — environmental challenges (predators, disease, food scarcity) that determine survival
  • Better adapted individuals have more offspring → advantageous traits become more common
  • Over many generations → populations change → eventually new species (speciation)
  • Charles Darwin proposed natural selection in "On the Origin of Species" (1859)
Evidence for evolution: Fossil record · Comparative anatomy (homologous structures) · DNA comparisons · Observed evolution (antibiotic resistance, Galapagos finches)

🌍 Ecosystems and Biomes

  • Ecosystem — community of organisms + their non-living environment interacting together
  • Biome — large-scale ecosystem defined by climate: tropical rainforest, desert, tundra, ocean, temperate forest, grassland
  • Abiotic factors — non-living: temperature, rainfall, light intensity, pH, wind
  • Biotic factors — living: predators, prey, competitors, disease, parasites
  • Habitat — where an organism lives. Niche — its role in the ecosystem
Tropical rainforests cover ~6% of Earth's surface but contain over 50% of plant and animal species. They also absorb huge amounts of CO₂ — vital for climate regulation.

🔄 Mitosis and Meiosis

Mitosis
1 cell → 2 genetically identical daughter cells. Chromosome number maintained (diploid, 2n). Used for growth, tissue repair, and asexual reproduction. Produces clones.
Meiosis
1 cell → 4 genetically unique gametes (sex cells). Chromosome number halved (haploid, n=23). Crossing over creates variation. Sperm (n=23) + egg (n=23) → zygote (2n=46).

🧪 Protein Synthesis

  • Transcription — in the nucleus: DNA template strand copied into messenger RNA (mRNA)
  • Translation — in the ribosome: mRNA sequence read in triplets (codons); each codon codes for one amino acid
  • 20 amino acids. Order determines protein's shape and function
  • Proteins include: enzymes (biological catalysts), hormones (insulin), structural proteins (collagen, keratin), antibodies
Mutation = change in DNA base sequence. Can alter the amino acid sequence → non-functional protein. Most mutations are harmless; some cause disease (e.g. sickle cell anaemia). Some are beneficial → evolution.

⚡ Nervous System

  • Sensory neuron — carries signals from receptors (skin, eye, nose) to CNS
  • Motor neuron — carries signals from CNS to effectors (muscles, glands)
  • Relay neuron — connects sensory and motor neurons (in CNS)
  • Reflex arc — receptor → sensory neuron → relay neuron (spinal cord) → motor neuron → effector. Faster than conscious thought — protective
  • Synapse — gap between neurons. Neurotransmitters (chemicals) carry signal across. e.g. acetylcholine, dopamine
Reflex example: Touch hot object → pain receptor → sensory neuron → spinal cord (reflex arc) → motor neuron → arm muscle contracts and pulls away. Brain receives signal AFTER you've already moved.

⚖️ Hormones vs Nervous System

  • Insulin (pancreas) — lowers blood glucose when it rises after eating. Opposite: glucagon raises blood glucose
  • Adrenaline (adrenal glands) — fight or flight response: increases heart rate, dilates airways, raises blood sugar
  • Testosterone (testes) / Oestrogen (ovaries) — sex hormones controlling secondary sexual characteristics and reproduction
  • ADH (pituitary) — controls water reabsorption in kidneys; regulates water balance
  • Type 1 diabetes — immune system destroys insulin-producing cells. Treated with insulin injections
Negative feedback: Blood glucose rises → pancreas releases insulin → glucose taken into cells → blood glucose falls → insulin production stops. This self-regulation maintains homeostasis.

🌱 Biodiversity and Conservation

  • Biodiversity — variety of species in an area; also includes genetic variation within species and habitat variety
  • High biodiversity = stable ecosystem (more food web connections = more resilient to change)
  • Habitat destruction (deforestation, urbanisation) is the #1 cause of species loss
  • Conservation methods: National parks and nature reserves · Captive breeding programmes · Seed banks (e.g. Svalbard) · Rewilding · International agreements (CITES)
  • Extinction is permanent — once gone, a species cannot be recovered (generally)
Ecosystem services provided by biodiversity: pollination of crops · water purification · carbon storage · soil fertility · medicines (penicillin from mould, aspirin from willow bark). Loss of biodiversity threatens all of these.

🔬 Biotechnology

  • Genetic engineering — cutting a gene from one organism and inserting it into another using restriction enzymes (cut) and ligase (join)
  • Insulin production — human insulin gene inserted into E. coli bacteria; bacteria ferment in vats and produce insulin at scale for diabetics
  • Golden Rice — rice genetically modified to produce beta-carotene (vitamin A precursor) to combat deficiency in developing countries
  • CRISPR-Cas9 — gene editing tool that can cut and modify DNA at precise locations; potential to cure genetic diseases
  • Stem cells — undifferentiated cells that can develop into any specialised cell type; used in medicine to repair damaged tissues
Ethical considerations: GM crops — could harm ecosystems, reduce biodiversity, or be monopolised by corporations. Designer babies — where is the line between treatment and enhancement? These questions have no single right answer.
