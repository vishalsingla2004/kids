🧊 States of Matter

  • Solid — fixed shape and fixed volume; particles vibrate in place
  • Liquid — fixed volume, takes the shape of its container; particles slide past each other
  • Gas — no fixed shape or volume; fills any container; particles move freely and fast
Key: Particles are always moving. The hotter the substance, the faster the particles move. Add enough heat to a solid → liquid → gas (melting and boiling). Remove heat → gas → liquid → solid (condensing and freezing).

⚛️ Atoms and Elements

  • Atom — the smallest particle of an element that still has the properties of that element
  • Element — a pure substance made of only one type of atom; cannot be broken down chemically
  • There are ~118 known elements listed in the Periodic Table
  • Nucleus — centre of atom; contains protons (+) and neutrons (neutral)
  • Electrons (−) orbit the nucleus in shells
Symbol examples: H = Hydrogen · O = Oxygen · C = Carbon · Fe = Iron · Na = Sodium · Au = Gold

📊 Periodic Table Basics

  • Periods — horizontal rows; period number = number of electron shells
  • Groups — vertical columns; elements in the same group have the same number of outer electrons → similar properties
  • Metals are on the left and centre; non-metals on the right
  • Group 1 = Alkali metals (very reactive). Group 18 = Noble gases (very unreactive)
Remember: Same group = similar chemical behaviour. Period 1 has just H and He. Over 100 elements are metals!

🔀 Mixtures vs Compounds

Mixture
Two or more substances physically combined. Each substance keeps its own properties. Can be separated (e.g. filtering, evaporation). No new substance formed.
Compound
Two or more elements chemically bonded. New substance with different properties. Difficult to separate without chemical reactions. Fixed ratio of atoms.
Famous example: Na (sodium) is explosive in water. Cl (chlorine) is a poisonous gas. Together as NaCl → safe table salt! Compounds can be totally different from their elements.

🔥 Physical vs Chemical Changes

Physical Change
No new substance formed. Usually reversible. Changes shape, size, or state. Examples: melting, freezing, dissolving, cutting, breaking.
Chemical Change
New substance(s) formed. Usually irreversible. Examples: burning, rusting, cooking, neutralisation. Clues: colour change, gas, temp change, precipitate.
Clues a chemical change happened: colour change · gas produced (bubbles) · temperature change · light produced · irreversible · new smell

⚗️ Acids and Bases

  • pH scale runs 0–14. pH 7 = neutral (pure water)
  • pH below 7 = acidic (lower number = stronger acid)
  • pH above 7 = alkaline/basic (higher number = stronger alkali)
  • Acids taste sour, corrode metals, turn litmus red
  • Alkalis feel soapy, turn litmus blue
Acid + Base → Salt + Water  (neutralisation)
Real life: Vinegar (acetic acid) removes limescale. Toothpaste (alkali) neutralises acid from bacteria. Bee sting (acid) → treat with baking soda (alkali).

⚖️ Chemical Formulas and Equations

  • Formula shows which atoms and how many: H₂O = 2 hydrogen + 1 oxygen
  • Subscript number = number of that atom (no number = 1)
  • Large number before formula = number of molecules: 2H₂O = 2 water molecules
  • Balanced equation — same number of each atom on both sides (law of conservation of mass)
2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O    (combustion of hydrogen)
Atoms are never created or destroyed in a chemical reaction — they are only rearranged. That is why equations must balance.

🔧 Separating Mixtures

  • Filtration — separates insoluble solid from liquid (e.g. sand from water)
  • Evaporation — heats solution until liquid evaporates, leaving dissolved solid (e.g. salt)
  • Distillation — separates liquids with different boiling points (e.g. water from alcohol); liquid boils, vapour condenses and is collected
  • Chromatography — separates dissolved substances carried different distances by a solvent (e.g. inks in pen)
Choice depends on the mixture: undissolved solid → filter. Dissolved solid → evaporate. Mixed liquids → distil. Dissolved dyes → chromatography.

🔩 Metals and Non-Metals

Exceptions: Mercury (Hg) is a metal that is liquid at room temperature. Graphite (a form of carbon) is a non-metal that conducts electricity — used in pencils and electrodes.

🔥 Reactions with Oxygen

  • Combustion — substance + oxygen → oxide + energy. Complete combustion (enough O₂): fuel → CO₂ + H₂O
  • Rusting — iron + oxygen + water → iron oxide (Fe₂O₃). Needs both O₂ and water. Slow oxidation
  • Thermal decomposition — heat breaks a compound into simpler substances (e.g. CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂)
  • Oxidation — gaining oxygen. Reduction = losing oxygen
Fire triangle: Remove any one of fuel, oxygen, or heat — fire goes out. That's the basis for all fire-fighting!

🔬 Atomic Structure

  • Protons (+) in nucleus — define the element (atomic number)
  • Neutrons (no charge) in nucleus — add mass
  • Electrons (−) in shells around nucleus
  • Shell filling order: 1st shell max 2, 2nd max 8, 3rd max 8
  • Neutral atom: number of protons = number of electrons
Atomic number = protons  |  Mass number = protons + neutrons
Example — Sodium (Na): Atomic number 11 → 11 protons, 11 electrons. Electron configuration: 2, 8, 1 (1 outer electron = very reactive!)

⚡ Reactivity Series of Metals

  • Metals higher in the series displace metals below them from solutions (displacement reaction)
  • Potassium (K) reacts violently with water, producing hydrogen gas and catching fire
  • Copper (Cu) does not react with water or dilute acids
  • Gold (Au) and silver (Ag) are so unreactive they are found pure in the earth — that is why ancient civilisations used them for jewellery
Displacement: Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu. Zinc is higher in the series so it displaces copper from copper sulfate solution. The blue solution goes colourless and copper metal deposits.

🔗 Chemical Bonding

Ionic Bonding
Metal + non-metal. Electrons transferred. Metal loses electrons → positive ion (cation). Non-metal gains electrons → negative ion (anion). Opposite charges attract. Forms giant ionic lattice.
Covalent Bonding
Non-metal + non-metal. Electrons shared between atoms. Both atoms achieve full outer shells. Forms molecules (H₂, H₂O, CO₂). Can be single, double, or triple bonds.
Metallic bonding — metal atoms lose outer electrons into a "sea of delocalised electrons". This sea of electrons is what makes metals good conductors of electricity and heat.

🔢 Mole Concept (Intro)

  • Mole — a counting unit for atoms/molecules, like "dozen" = 12, "mole" = 6.02 × 10²³
  • Avogadro's number = 6.02 × 10²³ (that is 602,000,000,000,000,000,000,000 particles!)
  • Molar mass = mass of one mole = atomic/molecular mass in grams
  • To find moles: moles = mass (g) ÷ molar mass (g/mol)
n = m / M    (moles = mass ÷ molar mass)
Example: How many moles in 36 g of water (H₂O, M = 18 g/mol)? n = 36 ÷ 18 = 2 moles. That is 2 × 6.02 × 10²³ = 1.204 × 10²⁴ molecules of water.

📈 Rates of Reaction

  • Concentration — more particles in same volume → more frequent collisions → faster reaction
  • Temperature — particles move faster, more energetic collisions → more exceed activation energy
  • Surface area — smaller pieces, larger surface area → more collisions possible (powder > lumps)
  • Catalyst — lowers activation energy, provides alternative pathway; not used up in the reaction
Collision theory: Reactions happen when particles collide with enough energy (≥ activation energy). Anything that increases collision frequency OR energy increases rate. Catalyst does both by providing a lower-energy path.

⚡ Electrolysis

  • Electrolysis — using electricity to decompose an ionic compound (when molten or dissolved)
  • Cathode (−) — cations (positive ions) move here; gain electrons (reduction); metal or H₂ deposited
  • Anode (+) — anions (negative ions) move here; lose electrons (oxidation); O₂ or Cl₂ released
  • Electrolyte — must be ionic and free to move (molten or aqueous solution)
Uses: Extracting aluminium from molten Al₂O₃ · Electroplating (thin metal coating) · Making chlorine and sodium hydroxide (chlor-alkali process) · Producing hydrogen fuel

🛢️ Organic Chemistry Basics

  • Hydrocarbons — organic molecules containing only carbon and hydrogen
  • Alkanes — single C−C bonds only (saturated). General formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₂
  • Alkenes — contain one C=C double bond (unsaturated). General formula CₙH₂ₙ. More reactive than alkanes
  • Crude oil = mixture of hydrocarbons. Separated by fractional distillation
Combustion: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + energy
Test for alkene: Add bromine water — turns from orange-brown to colourless if a C=C double bond is present (bromine adds across the double bond).

🔩 Alloys

  • Alloy — a mixture of a metal with one or more other elements (usually metals)
  • Different-sized atoms disrupt the regular lattice → layers cannot slide → harder and stronger
  • Steel = iron + carbon (up to ~2%). Harder than pure iron. Used in construction, tools
  • Bronze = copper + tin. Harder than copper. Used in ancient tools, bells
  • Brass = copper + zinc. Harder, more resistant. Used in musical instruments, plumbing
  • Solder = tin + lead (or tin + silver). Low melting point. Used in electronics
Why not use pure metals? Pure metals are often too soft (e.g. pure gold is soft — 24k is pure, 18k is alloyed with other metals to make jewellery harder). Alloying tailors properties for specific uses.
